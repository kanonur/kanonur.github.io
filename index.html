Projects

Academic Projects

This group includes the main academic projects, laboratory work, and course-based engineering studies developed during my Master's degree, where materials science, characterisation, modelling, and design were applied through structured technical work.

Master's Thesis — Thermochromic VO₂ Materials and Coatings for Smart Window Applications

At the centre of my thesis is a question that is both scientific and practical: how can VO₂-based materials be processed into coatings that respond usefully to temperature while still remaining suitable for real smart-window applications? The work brings together functional behaviour, process choices, and optical evaluation, so the material is never treated in isolation from its final use.

  • Project direction: VO₂ is studied here as a thermochromic system for glazing concepts that aim to regulate solar input in a temperature-dependent way.
  • What I am working on: synthesis and coating-oriented development are combined with the reading of optical and surface-sensitive results, allowing the material response to be evaluated through the lens of application.
  • Technical methods: the project draws on UV-Vis, FTIR, ellipsometry, and contact-angle analysis, together with experimental planning, data interpretation, and structured thesis writing in English.
  • Why it matters: more than a single materials study, the thesis reflects the point where functional materials research meets process strategy and application-driven engineering.
Nanostructured Materials — Nanoparticle Synthesis, Thin-Film Deposition, and Functional Characterisation

Nanostructured materials became meaningful to me when synthesis, deposition, and characterisation started to read like parts of the same story rather than separate laboratory steps. This work followed that logic closely, moving from the preparation of nanomaterials to thin-film formation and then to the interpretation of how those choices shaped final behaviour.

  • Project direction: the work covered nanoparticle synthesis, nanostructure processing, and thin-film deposition, with constant attention to how nanoscale decisions appear later in measurable properties.
  • What I worked with: chemical preparation routes, dip-based film deposition, and laboratory reporting were all part of the workflow, supported by structural and optical interpretation.
  • Technical methods: the environment around this work was closely tied to SEM, XRD, UV-Vis, FTIR, and the reading of structure–property relations in functional nanomaterials.
  • What it strengthened: the project strengthened a more integrated way of thinking, where processing, evidence, and interpretation support each other rather than sit in separate boxes.
Materials Selection and Design — Advanced Ceramics for Valve Components under Wear and Thermal Constraints

Material selection becomes genuinely interesting when the choice has consequences. In this case, the problem revolved around valve-related service demands, where ceramic candidates had to be judged not by reputation or familiarity, but by how well they could respond to wear, thermal effects, and engineering constraints taken together.

  • Project direction: ceramic alternatives to alumina were compared for service conditions involving wear resistance, thermal effects, and broader application-level requirements.
  • What I worked through: performance-based selection logic, weighted property comparisons, and explicit technical justification replaced intuitive ranking and made the decision process more rigorous.
  • Technical methods: Ashby-style reasoning, performance indices, trade-off analysis, and structured technical writing were used to support a defensible final recommendation.
  • What it strengthened: the project sharpened the link between materials knowledge and engineering judgment, especially when priorities, compromises, and design constraints all have to be weighed at once.
Finite Element Method and Structural Integrity — Numerical Modelling, Stress Analysis, Fatigue, and Fracture

Mechanics became far more concrete once it could be tested through modelling. This body of work brought together structural reasoning, failure logic, and numerical analysis, turning abstract mechanical questions into modelled problems that could be checked, compared, and interpreted with more discipline.

  • Project direction: the work moved across stress analysis, failure criteria, fatigue-sensitive behaviour, fracture-oriented reasoning, and mechanical verification in engineering contexts.
  • What I worked through: assignments combined theoretical mechanics with model-based thinking, including discretization logic, boundary conditions, and the interpretation of structural response.
  • Technical methods: Python-based numerical work, FEM reasoning, convergence awareness, and ANSYS-linked structural analysis references all shaped the way these problems were approached.
  • What it strengthened: the result was a stronger understanding of modelling as a method of engineering judgment, not just a computational routine.
Composite Materials — Constituent Selection, Laminate Behaviour, and Application-Oriented Case Studies

Composites stood out because they made design feel systemic. Performance was never traced back to one constituent alone; instead, it emerged from the interaction between reinforcement, matrix, interface, architecture, and manufacturing route. That made the work less about isolated properties and more about how a material system is built.

  • Project direction: the work focused on fibre-reinforced systems, laminate behaviour, failure mechanisms, and application contexts where composite choices are made for very specific performance reasons.
  • What I worked through: case-study and group work connected constituent selection, anisotropy, load transfer, laminate response, and manufacturing logic with engineering interpretation.
  • Technical methods: the main outputs were technical report writing, case-based evaluation, and structure–property reasoning informed by composite mechanics and fabrication approaches.
  • What it strengthened: the project deepened the ability to read a composite as an engineered architecture, where interface quality and process choices matter as much as the raw ingredients themselves.
Quality and Metrology — Dimensional Inspection, Point Clouds, Surface Analysis, and Computed Tomography

Measurement became most interesting when it stopped being just about numbers. In metrology, every dataset carries a decision behind it: whether geometry is acceptable, whether a surface is consistent, whether a process is under control. That is what made this area compelling, especially as the work moved between dimensional inspection, point-cloud workflows, surface analysis, and volumetric evaluation.

  • Project direction: the work included optical CMM inspection, handheld and laser-based scanning, CAD comparison, roughness evaluation, optical surface metrology, and X-ray computed tomography.
  • What I worked with: articulated-arm CMM workflows, multisensor inspection platforms, point-cloud processing, and surface-analysis methods formed the practical backbone of the metrology labs.
  • Technical methods: direct exposure included Nikon MCAx, Creaform HandySCAN 3D Silver, Zeiss O-Inspect 322, confocal microscopy, focus-variation microscopy, and X-ray CT.
  • What it strengthened: the strongest outcome was a better understanding of how inspection logic and data interpretation support engineering decisions in quality-critical manufacturing.

ANSYS

My ANSYS self-training has been built around simulation workflow, mesh preparation, structural analysis, heat-flow analysis, impact simulation, buckling studies, and topology-optimization exercises. The work was not limited to pressing software commands; it focused on understanding how geometry cleanup, boundary-condition definition, mesh quality, solver setup, and result interpretation affect whether a simulation can be treated as technically meaningful.

  • Workbench workflow: I practised the general finite-element workflow in ANSYS Workbench, including model import, material definition, contact and support setup, load application, solution control, and post-processing of stress, deformation, and equivalent-result fields.
  • Geometry and preprocessing: the training also covered practical model-preparation steps such as simplifying geometry, identifying problematic regions before meshing, preparing analysis-ready bodies, and checking whether the model setup matched the intended physical problem.
  • Meshing focus: a major part of the work was connected to meshing strategy. I worked with global and local sizing, element-quality awareness, refinement around holes and curved regions, structured versus unstructured mesh logic, and the effect of mesh density on result stability.
  • Analysis examples: my practice included meshing studies, static structural stress analysis, buckling-oriented exercises, heat-flow analysis, impact-simulation work, and topology-optimization workflows. The objective was not only to obtain a coloured plot, but to understand what the result says about load path, stiffness, thermal response, deformation behaviour, material distribution, and design feasibility.
  • What I gained: this training strengthened my ability to move from raw geometry to a defensible simulation setup. It also made me more critical of simulation results, especially regarding mesh quality, boundary conditions, local stress peaks, and whether a result should be trusted or treated only as a preliminary design indication.

  • What I covered: the course developed a practical base in the Fusion 360 interface, navigation, sketching tools, parametric part modelling, feature creation, editing operations, hole and thread tools, projection tools, section views, and part-property reading such as mass, volume, and density.
  • Design workflow: beyond individual commands, the training followed the logic of building usable mechanical models from sketches to solid parts, then moving into assembly-oriented thinking, modelling assignments, and the preparation of 2D engineering drawings aligned with technical-design practice.

  • What I gained: this training helped me become more confident with CAD structure and modelling sequence. It strengthened my ability to think in terms of clean geometry, controllable features, and design intent rather than approaching modelling as a series of isolated commands.
