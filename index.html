    • Nanostructured Materials — Dr. Elena Colusso, Prof. Alessandro Martucci

      Nanostructured Materials was organized around a practical chain of reasoning: how nanomaterials are made, how they are processed into functional forms, and how their structure and properties are verified experimentally. Instead of separating synthesis, deposition, and characterisation into isolated topics, the course treated them as consecutive stages of the same materials-engineering workflow.

      • What I learned: the course built a working understanding of the chemical synthesis of nanoparticles across metallic, semiconducting, and oxide systems, together with the main physical properties and application logic associated with nanostructured materials.
      • What I worked with: thin-film deposition methods such as dipping, spinning, and capillary-flow processing were part of the course, so nanomaterials were studied not only as powders or dispersions but also as systems that must be transferred onto surfaces in a controlled way.
      • What I used: structural and functional characterisation was a central part of the training. The course required reading the results of nanomaterials through laboratory observation, reporting, and the connection between processing choices and final measurable behaviour.
      • What I gained: the laboratory component made the course more concrete, because students had to synthesize and characterize different kinds of nanomaterials directly rather than remaining at the level of theory alone.
      • Broader exposure: educational visits to nanotechnology research laboratories extended the course beyond the classroom and showed how the same synthesis and characterisation logic operates in research environments.

      What I gained most from the course was an applied understanding of nanomaterials as real engineering systems. It strengthened my ability to move from preparation to deposition, then from deposition to characterisation and interpretation, which is exactly the logic needed in functional coatings, nanoparticle-based materials, and laboratory-scale nanotechnology work.

    • Fundamentals of Nanoscience — Prof. Giovanni Mattei, Prof. Chiara Maccato, Prof. Moreno Meneghetti

      Fundamentals of Nanoscience established the physical and chemical basis needed to understand why materials begin to behave differently once their characteristic dimensions move into the nanoscale range. For the Materials Engineering track, the course combined the common nanoscience module with a second module focused on preparation routes, interfaces, and the controlled growth of nanostructures, linking theory, processing, and characterisation rather than treating them as separate topics.

      • Size-dependent behaviour: the course introduced the classification and general properties of nanostructured materials through quantum confinement, electronic-size effects, and the equations used to describe how reduced dimensions modify material response.
      • Thermodynamics, nucleation, and growth: particular attention was given to thermodynamic size effects, the Gibbs–Thomson relation, nucleation, and growth regimes such as diffusion-limited aggregation and Ostwald ripening, making it clear that nanoscale structure is strongly governed by energetic competition and kinetic pathways.
      • Optical and plasmonic response: metallic nanostructures were studied through their optical behaviour, including non-interacting and interacting plasmonic systems, together with Mie theory and related approaches used to interpret their light–matter interaction.
      • Synthesis and structural control: the preparation of nanostructures was approached from both top-down and bottom-up routes, with stronger emphasis on the latter. The course covered colloidal methods, templating effects, self-assembly, self-organization, core–shell systems, and the formation of nanoparticles, nanowires, nanotubes, thin films, self-assembled monolayers, and Langmuir–Blodgett films.
      • Interfaces, defects, and processing routes: another important part of the course dealt with structural stabilization, defects in nanoscale materials, controlled porosity, coherent and semi-coherent interfaces, epitaxial and pseudomorphic growth, and thin-film techniques such as CVD, MBE, PVD, ALE, and PLD.
      • Characterisation and verification: electron microscopy was treated as a key tool for reading nanostructures, while nanostructures embedded in solid matrices and ion implantation were used to connect real processing routes with the verification of nucleation and growth models.

      What made the course especially valuable was that it did not present nanoscience as a collection of isolated effects. It showed how confinement, thermodynamics, interfaces, synthesis strategy, and optical behaviour all belong to the same engineering logic. That made the course directly relevant to applications in photonics, plasmonics, catalysis, and other functional nanotechnologies where controlling matter at very small scale determines final performance.

    • Biopolymers Engineering — Prof. Giovanna Brusatin

      Biopolymers Engineering was structured around two demanding application areas rather than a generic overview of polymers. The course showed that polymer design changes substantially when the goal is environmental sustainability on one side and biomedical performance on the other.

      • Environmental and industrial perspective: bio-based plastics were examined through renewable feedstocks, natural polymers, circular-economy thinking, market relevance, and production routes based on fermentation and microbial synthesis.
      • Biomedical perspective: the course then moved into polymeric biomaterials, comparing synthetic and natural systems for health-related use and distinguishing bioinert polymers from bioresorbable ones.
      • Material families and behaviour: hydrogels received particular attention, not only as soft polymer networks but as systems whose synthesis, structure, and mechanical response have to be controlled for function.
      • Advanced biomedical context: the framework extended to regenerative medicine, tissue engineering, cell culture, stem cells, organoids, 3D biofabrication, and polymeric micro- and nanoparticles used as carriers in advanced therapeutic strategies.
      • Scientific working method: journal club discussions and scientific paper analysis were part of the course, so reading, interpreting, and presenting literature formed part of the technical training itself.

      What made the course especially useful was the way it connected sustainability-oriented polymer engineering with the logic of biomedical materials design. It made clear that the same polymer knowledge base can lead to circular industrial materials or to precisely controlled systems for therapy and regeneration, depending on how feedstock, structure, degradability, and processing are managed.

    • Polymer Processing and Recycling — Dr. Carlo Boaretti

      Polymer Processing and Recycling treated polymers as process-sensitive materials whose final behaviour depends not only on chemistry, but also on additives, melt behaviour, manufacturing conditions, and end-of-life strategy. The course was built around a practical industrial logic: how polymers are formulated, how they are processed in the molten state, how defects and degradation are controlled, and how materials are identified and recovered once they become waste.

      • What I learned: the course covered the main families of polymer additives and their role during processing and final use, including stabilizers, antioxidants, UV-protection systems, flame retardants, plasticizers, mineral fillers, and fibrous reinforcements such as glass, carbon, and Kevlar.
      • What I worked with: a major part of the course focused on polymer rheology and rheometry, so molten polymers were studied through viscosity, viscoelastic behaviour, power-law response, and the effects of molecular weight, temperature, and pressure. This also included tools such as capillary rheometry, Melt Flow Index, dilute-solution viscometry, and the basics of elongational flow.
      • What I used: the processing side was developed through extrusion, twin-screw extrusion, injection moulding, cooling behaviour, PvT curves, moulding defects, and other manufacturing routes for thermoplastics and thermosets, including thermoforming, rotational moulding, injection stretch blow moulding, reaction injection moulding, and resin transfer moulding.
      • What I gained: the course made it much clearer how formulation, degradation, rheology, and processing are linked. It also gave me a more realistic view of how engineering decisions are made when a polymer must meet both technical and production-related requirements.
      • Recycling perspective: another important part of the course dealt with polymer identification and sorting, legislative context, and recycling strategies such as mechanical recycling, chemical recycling, and waste-to-energy approaches, with concrete examples involving PET, polyolefins, PVC, and polyurethanes.

      What I gained most from the course was a more complete engineering view of polymers across their full life cycle. It strengthened my ability to connect formulation, molten-state behaviour, process selection, product quality, and recycling strategy instead of treating them as separate topics.

    • Technology of Metals — Prof. Andrea Zambon

      Technology of Metals treated metallic materials through the full engineering route that connects alloy choice, microstructural control, deformation, joining, surface engineering, and manufacturing process selection. The course was built around a practical logic: a metal component cannot be understood only through its nominal alloy, because its final behaviour depends on the treatments, surface state, joining route, and production path used before service.

      • What I learned: the course developed a stronger basis for selecting the main metallic materials used in engineering, with particular attention to steels, aluminium alloys, titanium alloys, copper alloys, and superalloys. The central idea was not just to know the material families, but to understand how their use depends on microstructure and treatment history.
      • What I worked through: a major part of the course focused on steels and their treatments, including classification, designation, annealing, hardening, hardenability, tempering, cryogenic treatments, surface hardening, and thermochemical treatments such as carburizing, nitriding, carbonitriding, nitrocarburizing, and borurization.
      • What I used: the course connected metallurgy directly with manufacturing technologies. It covered welding from metallurgical and microstructural viewpoints, including defect formation and remedies; powder metallurgy with powder production, powder and compact properties, sintering, and post-sintering treatments; and the mechanisms of cold and hot plastic deformation together with recovery of work-hardened structures.
      • What I gained: surface engineering became much clearer through topics such as surface preparation, chemical conversion treatments, galvanic treatments, electroless deposition, metallization of polymers, anodizing of aluminium, thin-film deposition through PVD and CVD, and thick-film deposition by spray techniques. This made it easier to see how surface state is engineered, not merely added at the end.
      • Broader engineering view: the course strengthened the link between metallic materials and real component manufacturing by showing how bulk alloy behaviour, surface modification, joining, and processing decisions all contribute to final performance and service reliability.

      What I gained most from the course was a more complete engineering view of metals as process-dependent systems. It improved my ability to connect heat treatment, deformation, joining, powder-based production, and coatings within one coherent framework, which is essential when selecting or processing metallic materials for demanding applications.

    • Science and Technology of Ceramics — Prof. Alessandro Martucci

      Science and Technology of Ceramics treated ceramics as a complete engineering family, from crystal structure and defect chemistry to processing, properties, and final applications. The course was built around a clear logic: ceramic behaviour cannot be understood by looking only at composition or only at processing, because structure, defects, fabrication route, and service conditions are tightly connected.

      • What I learned: the course began with the structure of ionic and covalent ceramics, then moved into point defects, Kröger–Vink notation, stoichiometric and non-stoichiometric defects, diffusion, and ionic and electronic conductivity. This gave the physical basis needed to understand why ceramics show specific functional and structural behaviour.
      • What I worked through: a major part of the course focused on the scientific basis of ceramic processing, including raw materials, powders, powder properties, additives, mixtures, forming technologies, drying, and heat treatment. Sintering, grain growth, and pore elimination were treated as core mechanisms rather than as simple production steps.
      • What I used: the mechanical side covered elastic modulus, brittle fracture, G and R functions, stress-intensity concepts such as KI and KIc, crack stability, R-curves, toughening mechanisms, subcritical crack growth, dynamic fatigue, creep, proof testing, lifetime prediction, and statistical design approaches such as Weibull-based reasoning.
      • What I gained: the course made thermal behaviour and reliability much clearer by connecting melting point, thermal stresses, and service conditions with the intrinsic fragility of ceramics and the engineering strategies used to improve performance.
      • Broader materials view: the final part of the course extended the same logic to traditional ceramics, refractories, structural and functional advanced ceramics, cements, concrete, and ternary diagrams, so ceramics were never treated as a narrow niche but as a wide class of materials with very different industrial roles.

      What I gained most from the course was a more rigorous way of thinking about ceramics as engineered materials rather than simply brittle materials. It strengthened my ability to connect defect chemistry, processing route, fracture behaviour, thermal response, and final application requirements within one coherent materials-engineering framework.

    • Composite Materials — Prof. Giorgia Franchin, Prof. Lisa Biasetto

      Composite Materials treated composites as engineered systems whose performance depends on the interaction between constituents, architecture, interfaces, and processing. The course built the logic needed to understand why a laminate, a short-fibre composite, or a particulate system behaves differently under load, temperature, and service conditions.

      • What I learned: the course covered the main reinforcement families—carbon, glass, polymeric, silicon carbide, oxide, whiskers, nanotubes, and particulates—together with thermosetting, thermoplastic, metal, and ceramic matrices, and the structural differences between long-fibre, short-fibre, and particulate systems.
      • What I worked through: a major part of the course focused on load transfer, property maps, merit indices, elastic constants, anisotropy, lamina description, laminate response, and modelling approaches such as shear-lag and Eshelby-type methods for short-fibre and particulate composites.
      • What I used: the course moved beyond stiffness into interfacial bonding, bond-strength control, laminate strength, off-axis failure, fracture mechanics, toughness, thermal stresses, thermal cycling, creep, and thermal conduction, so mechanical reasoning was always tied to realistic service behaviour.
      • What I gained: the broader view of composites became clearer through topics such as porous materials, bio-composites like wood and bone, cellulose-based reinforcements, recycling, nanoscale reinforcement, and fabrication routes for polymer-, metal-, and ceramic-matrix systems.

      What I gained most from the course was the ability to read composite performance as the result of system design rather than as a simple property list. It strengthened my understanding of how constituent selection, laminate architecture, interface quality, and manufacturing route work together in real engineering applications.

    • Introduction to the Finite Element Method — Prof. Angelo Simone

      Introduction to the Finite Element Method was built as a bridge between theory and implementation. The course treated FEM not as software use, but as a structured way of turning a continuum mechanics problem into a discrete numerical model that can be written, checked, and interpreted critically.

      • What I learned: the course moved from discrete systems such as spring assemblies to governing equations and variational principles, clarifying how a mechanical problem passes from strong form to weak form before any element formulation begins.
      • What I worked through: the theoretical side included Hilbert and Sobolev spaces, the Bubnov–Galerkin method, spatial discretization, convergence conditions such as Céa's lemma, and error-estimation logic with h, p, hp, and r refinement strategies.
      • What I used: the element-level work covered direct stiffness-matrix formulations for bars and planar elements, rotation matrices for trusses, isoparametric formulations in 1D, 2D, and 3D, Gauss–Lagrange numerical integration, and sparse assembly procedures.
      • What I gained: the coding path extended from linear small-strain elasticity to incremental problems, diffusion, consistent linearization, Newton–Raphson procedures, material nonlinearity, and local damage, so the course made clear how the FEM framework evolves once simple linear assumptions no longer hold.
      • Practical tools: students were guided in writing FEM code for 1D, 2D, and 3D linear-elasticity problems, with mesh generation in Gmsh and post-processing in ParaView, which made the numerical workflow concrete rather than abstract.

      What I gained most from the course was a more disciplined way of thinking about modelling itself. It strengthened my ability to connect mechanics, mathematics, coding, and interpretation, and to treat numerical results as something that has to be justified and understood, not simply accepted.

    • Materials Structural Integrity — Mattia Manzolaro, Giuseppe Zullo

      Materials Structural Integrity treated mechanical reliability as a question of method rather than only of formulas. The course connected theoretical mechanics with applied structural reasoning, so the central issue was always how to judge whether a component remains reliable under static and dynamic service loading.

      • What I learned: the course began from internal reactions, stress state, strain state, and stress-component calculation, building the base needed to read loaded structures correctly before moving into more advanced integrity problems.
      • What I worked through: Mohr circles, failure criteria, mechanical characterisation, and stress concentration factors were used to understand when local stresses remain acceptable and when they become critical.
      • What I used: the course addressed classical fatigue, low-cycle fatigue, welded joints under static and dynamic loading, fracture mechanics, and curved beams, so the analysis moved from basic members to more critical structural situations where damage and discontinuities matter.
      • What I gained: a brief introduction to finite elements and guided calculation-laboratory work with finite-element codes showed how analytical reasoning and numerical support can work together in structural verification.

      What I gained most from the course was a clearer engineering view of reliability. It improved my ability to connect stress analysis, fatigue, fracture, welded structures, and numerical support into one coherent way of judging structural safety.

    • Quality in Manufacturing Engineering — Prof. Sofia Catalucci

      Quality in Manufacturing Engineering approached metrology as an engineering tool for controlling production, not as a purely descriptive measurement activity. The course was built around a clear industrial logic: how dimensions, form, surface, and geometrical specifications are verified, how measuring systems support quality assurance, and how reliable data can be used to monitor manufacturing processes in a technically meaningful way.

      • What I learned: the course introduced the foundations of metrology in manufacturing, with strong emphasis on geometrical product specifications, dimensional verification, surface characterisation, and the role of measurement in industrial quality control, including additive-manufacturing contexts.
      • What I worked with: a major part of the course dealt with industrial geometrical metrology through coordinate measuring machines, optical coordinate measuring instruments, computed tomography for industrial inspection, smart inspection systems, statistical process control, and point-cloud processing with comparison to CAD models and meshes.
      • What I used: the laboratory side made the course especially concrete. Hands-on work included articulated-arm CMM systems with laser scanning, metrological computed tomography, optical 3D profilers based on confocal, focus variation, and white-light interferometry approaches, digital roughness testers, and ZEISS Inspect for data analysis.
      • What I gained: the course made it much clearer how measurement data become engineering decisions. It strengthened my ability to connect dimensional control, surface texture, form verification, and data analysis with process monitoring and manufacturing quality.
      • Broader perspective: the emphasis on geometrical verification, surface topography, and digital comparison workflows showed that modern metrology is not limited to checking single dimensions, but extends to full-shape analysis, texture evaluation, and integrated quality management.

      What I gained most from the course was a more practical understanding of how metrology supports industrial reliability. It helped me see measurement systems not as passive instruments, but as active tools for verification, troubleshooting, and process improvement in quality-critical manufacturing environments.

    • Materials Selection and Design — Prof. Enrico Bernardo

      Materials Selection and Design approached material choice as a structured engineering decision rather than as a matter of habit or preference. The course was built around the idea that component performance depends jointly on function, geometry, process constraints, and environmental impact, so material selection was always treated alongside design logic.

      • What I learned: the course began with Ashby-style materials-property charts and performance indices, showing how to isolate the material contribution inside a performance equation and how to screen candidates on a rational basis.
      • What I worked through: selection problems with multiple constraints or competing objectives were handled through graphical and numerical methods, including penalization curves, generalized performance functions, and weighting factors.
      • What I used: an important part of the course dealt with the interaction between material properties and geometry. Shape factors made it clear that performance can often be improved not only by changing material, but also by redesigning the component in a process-feasible way.
      • What I gained: the same selection logic was extended to composite systems, reinforcing phases, cellular materials, and environmental selection, including eco-audit thinking and the use of CES EduPack for applied materials-selection problems.

      What I gained most from the course was a method for turning vague design requirements into defendable engineering choices. It strengthened my ability to move from equations to indices, from indices to charts, and from charts to justified material and shape decisions while keeping trade-offs and manufacturability visible.

    • Solid State Physics — Prof. Andrea Gasparotto

      Solid State Physics built the physical framework needed to understand why materials behave the way they do at the microscopic level before those behaviours appear later as electrical, thermal, magnetic, or device-level properties. The course moved from the foundations of modern physics into the description of electrons in solids, so it connected abstract theory with the material phenomena that matter in engineering applications.

      • What I learned: the first part of the course introduced the basic principles of modern physics, especially the theoretical and experimental foundations of quantum theory. Wave mechanics, the wave function, and its probabilistic meaning were treated as the starting point for understanding matter beyond classical intuition.
      • What I worked through: the course then moved to multi-atom solid systems and to the behaviour of electrons in a periodic crystal lattice. This gave the basis for understanding energy bands and for reading solids not simply as collections of atoms, but as systems whose electronic structure governs their macroscopic properties.
      • What I used: band theory was applied to the electrical properties of conductors and semiconductors, and the course also addressed the magnetic and thermal properties of solids. That made the theoretical part much more concrete, because the same concepts were repeatedly connected to observable material behaviour.
      • What I gained: the final part extended this logic to semiconductor devices, including their operating principles and fabrication technologies. This helped me see how fundamental solid-state concepts move directly into technological applications rather than remaining only at theoretical level.
      • Broader perspective: the course also required working through straightforward numerical applications, so the physical ideas were not treated only qualitatively but also through practical problem-solving.

      What I gained most from the course was a deeper physical intuition for advanced materials. It strengthened my ability to connect quantum foundations, crystal periodicity, band structure, and device behaviour in one coherent line of reasoning, which is essential when working with semiconductors and other functional material systems.

    • Sports Engineering and Rehabilitation Devices — Prof. Nicola Petrone

      Sports Engineering and Rehabilitation Devices approached engineering from the point where the human body, movement analysis, instrumentation, and device performance meet. The course was built around a practical logic: before a sport or rehabilitation device can be improved, its function has to be translated into measurable biomechanical parameters, captured with the right tools, and interpreted through a structured experimental method.

      • What I learned: the course began from the fundamentals needed to read human motion quantitatively, including basic anatomy and physiology of the musculoskeletal system, quantitative anthropometry, segment and joint kinematics, and the modelling of equilibrium and motion of body segments.
      • What I worked with: movement analysis was a central part of the course. Gait and running analysis, phase recognition, temporal diagrams of kinematic, kinetic, and physiological quantities, normalization in time or frequency domain, and the use of biomechanical models for joint and muscle equilibrium were all treated as practical tools rather than abstract theory.
      • What I used: the instrumentation side was especially concrete. The course worked with motion-capture systems, inertial sensors, force platforms, pressure insoles and mats, electromyography, strain-gauge multi-component load cells, musculoskeletal simulation software, and data-analysis methods used to evaluate body–equipment interaction during real use.
      • What I gained: the course made it much clearer how performance, comfort, and safety requirements of sport and rehabilitation devices can be converted into engineering variables, measured experimentally, and evaluated with statistical reasoning. It also strengthened my understanding of how research questions are formulated and answered through structured testing.
      • Broader application view: the framework extended to sport equipment, orthoses, prostheses, rehabilitation and training machines, assistive technologies, safety standards, standard test methods, and smart sensing systems, so the course connected biomechanics with product evaluation and device design in a very direct way.

      What I gained most from the course was a more applied understanding of biomechanics as an engineering discipline. It improved my ability to connect human movement, sensor systems, experimental design, data interpretation, and device evaluation within one coherent workflow, which is especially valuable when working on performance-oriented or human-centred engineering problems.

Master's Thesis — Thermochromic VO₂ Materials and Coatings for Smart Window Applications

At the centre of my thesis is a question that is both scientific and practical: how can VO₂-based materials be processed into coatings that respond usefully to temperature while still remaining suitable for real smart-window applications? The work brings together functional behaviour, process choices, and optical evaluation, so the material is never treated in isolation from its final use.

  • Project direction: VO₂ is studied here as a thermochromic system for glazing concepts that aim to regulate solar input in a temperature-dependent way.
  • What I am working on: synthesis and coating-oriented development are combined with the reading of optical and surface-sensitive results, allowing the material response to be evaluated through the lens of application.
  • Technical methods: the project draws on UV-Vis, FTIR, ellipsometry, and contact-angle analysis, together with experimental planning, data interpretation, and structured thesis writing in English.
  • Why it matters: more than a single materials study, the thesis reflects the point where functional materials research meets process strategy and application-driven engineering.
